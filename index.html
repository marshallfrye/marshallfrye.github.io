About Me

Marshall B. Frye  ·  Georgia Tech

I recently earned my PhD in Materials Science and Engineering from Georgia Tech. My research centers on a single underlying question: how can we control defects and interfaces to design emergent electronic materials and devices?

That question has taken me from growing thin films with monolayer precision and uncovering interlayer epitaxy, to writing phase-field simulations to elucidate the fundamental mechanisms of defects in enhancing device performance. The work has produced 11 peer-reviewed papers, 11 conference presentations, and recognition from MRS, AVS, and Acta Materialia.

Research Focus

Engineering Polar
Materials

I work at the intersection of synthesis, characterization, and phase-field simulation to understand what governs ferroelectric behavior, and how to use that understanding to build better materials for memory and energy applications.

Defect-mediated switching in Hf0.5Zr0.5O2

01 / Interface & Defect Engineering

Defect-Controlled Switching in HZO

Oxygen vacancies and interfacial charge do not just degrade devices. They control how polarization switches. In Hf0.5Zr0.5O2, I have mapped how defect populations set the internal bias, coercive field, and remanent polarization.

PLD growth & ferroelectric switching in 2D SnSe

02 / 2D Ferroelectrics

SnSe at the 2D Limit

SnSe is a van der Waals material with predicted structural transitions approaching the 2D limit. I developed wafer-scale PLD deposition with monolayer precision and reported the first bulk ferroelectric switching (P-E and PUND) measurements on this system.

Epitaxial stabilization & multiferroic properties of ScFeO3

03 / Hexagonal Ferrites

Metastable Rare-Earth Oxides

Hexagonal ferrites have unique potential for multiferroic and polar photovoltaic applications. I used interfacial engineering to elucidate the stabilization mechanism of ScFeO3 and expanded the approach to conductive substrates needed for devices.

Oxide-based memory & photodetector devices

04 / Photovoltaics & Memory

Functional Devices

Ferroic materials are not only exciting for their exotic physics, but also for emergent devices. By tuning material properties through defects and interface engineering, ferroic materials display unique promise for memory, sensing, and photodetector applications.

Research Agenda

Where My Research
is Going

As devices are scaled down, interfaces and defects increasingly dominate over bulk properties. My goal is a unified framework in which these effects are tunable parameters governing electronic behavior, enabling the stabilization, modulation, and enhancement of ferroelectricity and other emergent properties across diverse material systems.

01

Defect-Control in Emerging Ferroelectric Memory

Wake-up, imprint, and fatigue in wurtzite and fluorite devices are typically attributed to defect formation and redistribution under cycling. Developing methods to understand and control these defects should make reliability for FeRAM and FeFET devices more tractable.

02

Emerging Multiferroic Materials

Ferroelectric, (anti)ferromagnetic, and ferroelasticity have been theoretically predicted across a range of materials; yet experimental realization of these materials has lagged. In order to enable magnetoelectric sensors, 2D MESO logic, and tunnel junctions I will use targetted thin film deposition techniques to stabilize these materials and measure emergent phenomena.

03

Polar Materials for Photodetection

With the right deposition approach, SnSe, ScFeO3, and LuFeO3 combine visible-light band gaps with spontaneous polarization. Systematic interlayer and substrate engineering should push bulk photovoltaic efficiency toward the theoretical ceiling, enabling ultrafast light detection with above-bandgap photovoltages.

04

Science Consultancy

My training covers synthesis, characterization, modeling, and grant strategy. That combination is useful for advising technology development programs, materials qualification work, or R&D roadmapping in the semiconductor and clean-energy sectors.
